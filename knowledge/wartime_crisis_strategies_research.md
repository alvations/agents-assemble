Research date: 2026-04-08

## 1. Historical Wartime & Crisis Strategies

### World War II (1939-1945)
- Dow gained 50%+ by war's end after initial dips (Poland invasion, Pearl Harbor)
- Beer/liquor sector: +723% windfall
- Small-cap value: 12% annualized (1939-1947)
- Value factor CAGR: 16%, Size factor: 11% (1941-1945)
- Energy, healthcare, commodities outperformed
- Defensive sectors beat broader market by 8.5% during conflicts
- Tobacco stocks: worst performers despite positive returns
- Source: CFA Institute, ETFStream

### Gulf War (1990-1991)
- Iraq invasion (Aug 2, 1990): S&P 500 declined 13.5% over 3 months
- One year after invasion: S&P 500 up 10.16%
- Oil surged 135% to ~$46/barrel, then collapsed
- After Desert Storm (Jan 17, 1991): oil crashed 33%, S&P jumped 3.7% same day
- Jan 15 - Feb 15, 1991: S&P 500 up 17.63% in 4 weeks
- Key insight: buy during uncertainty, sell into clarity
- Market focused on oil prices, recession risk, not daily battle updates
- Source: Berkshire Edge, Yahoo Finance, TrendSpider

### 1997 Asian Financial Crisis
- Hedge funds (Soros, LTCM) profited initially from currency devaluations
- LTCM lost $4.6B in <4 months from leverage + exposure
- NBER research: no evidence hedge funds caused the crisis
- Currency devaluation trades worked short-term, blew up long-term
- Source: NBER Working Papers 6427

### 2008 Financial Crisis
- Contrarian strategies were the big winners
- John Paulson: +$15B betting against housing via CDS
- Michael Burry: Scion Capital profited from subprime CDS
- Warren Buffett: Bank of America investment during max pessimism
- Key: fundamental analysis + conviction + patience
- Strict position sizing and stop-loss helped control risks
- Source: LuxAlgo, Lyn Alden, Investopedia

## 2. Asset Class Performance During Crises

### Gold During Wars
- WWII: $33.85 (1940) to $43 (1947), +27%
- Post-9/11 (2001): $271/oz, sparked decade-long bull to $1,800+
- Ukraine (2022): Spiked to $2,070 in March 2022
- General: Gold rises sharply at onset of every major conflict since 1979
- 15-40% surges during geopolitical crises
- Source: Hero Bullion, Macrotrends, USAGOLD

### Commodities During Wartime
- Oil: +50-300% during major conflicts (military demand + supply disruption)
  - Gulf War 1990: $15 to $40 (+167%)
  - Arab-Israeli 1973: $3 to $12 (+300%)
  - Ukraine 2022: Brent +75% Jan-Mar
- Wheat: +50% during Ukraine war (breadbasket disruption)
- Corn: +40% during Ukraine war
- Aluminum, copper, nickel surge with military production ramp-up
- Source: SpotMarketCap, Brookings, World Bank

### Safe Haven Comparison
- Government bonds: effective during macro/financial crises (lower inflation/rates)
- Short-term T-bills: best during crises (no duration risk)
- Long-term bonds: uncertain in rate-hiking environments
- Gold: strong safe haven across all major crises (1987, 1997, 2008)
- Gold's low correlation to equities/bonds makes it compelling hedge
- JPY and CHF outperformed long-duration Treasuries since 2022
- No single asset provides universal protection across all scenarios
- Source: ScienceDirect, Morningstar, State Street

## 3. Seasonal Patterns

### January Barometer
- "As January goes, so goes the year"
- Positive January: stocks finish up 86% of the time, avg gain 16%
- Overall accuracy: 62% since 1927, 78% direction match
- Negative January: only 54% accurate (barely better than coin flip)
- Caveat: S&P positive 75% of all years anyway (base rate issue)
- Recent decline: 55% win rate over past 9 years
- Source: Fidelity, Invesco, IndexBox

### Santa Claus Rally
- Last 5 trading days of December + first 2 of January
- S&P 500 avg gain: 1.3%, positive ~80% of time
- Best: 2008-2009 transition = +7.4% (crisis recovery)
- If rally fails: S&P averages -1% in next 3 months
- If rally succeeds: S&P averages +2.6% in next 3 months
- Source: CME Group, Britannica Money, LPL Research

### Sell in May and Go Away
- May-October avg gain: only 1.8% vs Nov-Apr much stronger
- BUT: $1,000 invested 1975 buy-and-hold = $340,000 vs sell-in-May = $64,000
- Only worked in 12 of past 50 years
- Modern digital markets don't follow old patterns
- Verdict: pattern exists but strategy fails vs buy-and-hold
- Source: LPL Research, Fidelity, IG International

### Triple Witching
- 3rd Friday of March, June, September, December
- Stock options + index futures + index options all expire same day
- S&P 500 avg return on triple witching day: -0.52%
- Full triple witching week avg: -0.53% vs +0.37% non-expiration weeks
- Volume nearly doubles but intraday volatility actually lower than other expiries
- Source: Wikipedia, Option Alpha, Britannica Money

### Presidential Election Cycle
- Year 3 (pre-election) historically strongest
- Since 1928: Year 3 positive 78% of time, avg +13.5% vs all-year avg 7.7%
- Since 1943: Dow and S&P 500 up 15% avg in Year 3
- Nasdaq: +28.8% avg in Year 3 since 1971
- Year 2 (midterm) typically weakest and most volatile
- Reason: incumbent administrations stimulate economy pre-election
- Caveat: may be statistical coincidence; earnings and Fed policy bigger drivers
- Source: Stock Trader's Almanac, SoFi, Bankrate

## 4. Event-Driven Strategies

### FOMC Announcement Effect
- Pre-FOMC drift: S&P 500 ~50 bps higher before announcement vs prior day open
- 1980-1993: 20 bps avg pre-FOMC returns; other days order of magnitude smaller
- Sharpe ratios of FOMC-only strategy: 0.75-1.04 across 5 major countries
- Counter-expectation trades: +4.5% gains around surprises
- Selling 15 days after surprises: 4x higher Sharpe than buy-and-hold
- Source: NY Fed Staff Report 512, Quantpedia, Chicago Booth

### Non-Farm Payroll (NFP)
- First Friday of each month, 8:30 AM ET
- Markets tend to surprise despite leading indicators
- Miss exceeding 100,000 jobs → major trend shifts lasting days/weeks
- V-shape pattern: initial spike often reverses in minutes/hours
- Breakout trading: enter on breakout of key level post-report
- Fade the spike: wait for overreaction, trade reversal
- Source: Quantified Strategies, Trade That Swing, FOREX.com

### IPO Lock-Up Expiration (180 Days)
- Lock-up typically expires 180 days after IPO
- Insiders can sell after expiration, often causing price drops
- Market prices in selling pressure: stocks often drop days BEFORE expiration
- Shorting seems obvious but market already expects it
- Research: bid-ask spreads actually improve post-expiration (Lockup Expiration, ScienceDirect)
- Tricky to trade because timing is already priced in
- Source: SoFi, CapitalXchange, ScienceDirect

### Post-Earnings Announcement Drift (PEAD)
- Ball & Brown (1968): stocks continue drifting in direction of surprise for months
- Hedge portfolio (long good news, short bad news): 5.1% risk-adjusted over 3 months = 20%+ annual
- Magnitude declining over time in developed markets (more efficient)
- Combined with investor attention: returns can exceed 6% quarterly
- Source: Quantpedia, Wikipedia (PEAD), ScienceDirect
